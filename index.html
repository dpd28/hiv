The Changing Landscape of HIV

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) is no longer the death sentence it was 20 years ago. Yet for over 1 million people currently living with HIV in the U.S. and the 40,000 new cases each year, HIV/AIDS remains an epidemic. The hardest hit region: The South. Home to a third of Americans the South constitutes more than 52% of new HIV infections. Today, poor people of color in rural areas without healthcare access or health insurance are the most vulnerable. The complex mix of stigma, bigotry, policy, and more cause experts to question if HIV for these communities will ever be resolved.

The Deep South is the Hardest Hit

More than half of new HIV diagnoses in 2017 are in the Southern U.S. Its scattered geography makes prevention and treatment difficult.

HIV Diagnosis is Rising for Young People of color

Historically, new HIV diagnosis rates seem to have plateaued. In 2017, rates for men remain stable (23.1) and rates for women have decreased (5.2) but for Black and Hispanic millennials, rates have increased.
